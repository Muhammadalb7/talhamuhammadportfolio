Assessment of Stress Level and Insomnia Among Young Adults

The study titled "Assessment of Stress Level and Insomnia Among Young Adults" aimed to explore the correlation between stress and insomnia in individuals aged 18 to 30 years, including university students and young professionals. Using validated tools—the Perceived Stress Scale (PSS-10) and the Insomnia Severity Index (ISI)—data were collected from 250 participants. SPSS v25 was used for statistical analysis, applying Pearson correlation, chi-square tests, and descriptive statistics. The results revealed that 72.8% of participants reported moderate to high stress, while 63.2% exhibited clinical levels of insomnia, ranging from subthreshold to severe. Only 14.8% reported both low stress and normal sleep patterns, highlighting the widespread prevalence of mental health-related sleep disturbances in this age group.

A strong positive correlation (r = 0.71, p < 0.001) was observed between stress and insomnia severity, confirming that individuals experiencing higher stress levels were significantly more likely to suffer from sleep-related issues. Gender-based analysis showed females had significantly higher stress and insomnia scores (p < 0.05), particularly those juggling academic responsibilities or caregiving roles. Academic pressure played a key role in worsening sleep patterns, especially during exam periods. Additionally, screen time exceeding five hours daily—especially at night—was associated with delayed sleep onset and poor sleep quality. Lifestyle factors such as caffeine intake after 6 PM, sedentary behavior, and irregular sleep routines further contributed to elevated insomnia scores. Conversely, participants engaging in mindfulness activities or physical exercise reported relatively better sleep despite moderate stress levels.

In conclusion, the study emphasizes that stress is a major predictor of insomnia among young adults, intensified by academic and digital pressures and poor sleep hygiene. It highlights the importance of implementing institutional stress-reduction strategies, sleep hygiene education, and mental health awareness programs. Recommendations include digital detox practices before bedtime, promoting regular exercise, and introducing structured counseling or wellness workshops in academic and work settings. Future research should expand with longitudinal data and biological stress markers such as cortisol levels for a more comprehensive understanding of the stress-insomnia dynamic.
